# Study 3 Stage Structure — Hiring Manager Version

Study 3 uses the same four-stage skeleton as Study 2 but shifts the participant's perspective from job seeker to hiring manager. Both studies use the same five fictional job postings and the same between-subjects wage visibility manipulation (Arm A: wages shown; Arm B: wages hidden). The stages below describe what is shared with Study 2 and what is new.

---

## Stage 1 — Rankings

Participants rank all five postings on five dimensions: perceived pay, employer investment, values alignment, learning and growth, and overall desirability. The ranking dimensions, instructions, helper text, and implementation logic are identical to Study 2 (see `ranking_task_screens.md`). The only difference is the framing: Study 3 participants are told to rank the postings "as you imagine a strong candidate would evaluate them" rather than from their own perspective. This framing makes Stage 1 responses comparable across the two studies — any difference in rankings reflects how hiring managers model job seeker preferences versus how job seekers actually rank. Wages are hidden during Stage 1 in both studies.

## Stage 2 — Hiring Manager Card Sort (New)

This is the core new element in Study 3. For each posting, participants select the three things they would most want to add or strengthen to make the posting more attractive to strong candidates. The card pool contains 12 cards organized into four types: Purpose (P1–P4), Good-Employer (G1–G3), Wage/Compensation (W1–W3), and Task/Role (T1–T2). This card sort is the supply-side parallel to the job seeker card sort in Study 2 — where Study 2 asks what signals job seekers respond to, Study 3 asks what signals hiring managers reach for. Wage visibility is applied per arm assignment: Arm A participants see salary ranges on the postings, Arm B participants see "Salary: Undisclosed." See `study3_card_sort_screen.md` for the full card pool and instructions.

## Stage 3 — Competition Element (New)

Participants are told that strong candidates are already in hiring processes at other firms and asked whether they would change each posting given this competition. For each posting where they choose to make a change, they select the single most important addition from the same 12-card pool used in Stage 2. This stage tests the mechanism identified in the field study: when facing competition for talent, do hiring managers managing purpose-claiming postings reach for wage-type additions more than those managing good-employer or neutral postings? See `study3_competition_screen.md` for the full screen text and recording instructions.

## Stage 4 — Bucket Sort (Shared)

Identical to Study 2. Participants sort a pool of phrases from the job postings into labeled categories. This stage provides a manipulation check and a direct measure of signal detection. See `bucket_sort_screen.md` for the full task description.
